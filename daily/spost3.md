# Sloths Have Algae Growing in Their Fur (And That's a Good Thing)

One of the most surprising facts about sloths is that their coarse, shaggy fur is essentially a miniature ecosystem. Tiny grooves in each hair shaft collect moisture and create the perfect environment for blue-green algae to grow. Far from being a hygiene problem, this algae is actually a brilliant adaptation. It gives the sloth a greenish tint that helps it blend into the forest canopy, providing natural camouflage from predators. Some researchers believe sloths may even lick and eat the algae off their own fur, getting a small nutritional boost in the process. But the fur-based ecosystem doesn't stop there — sloths also host moths, beetles, cockroaches, and fungi living in their coats. The sloth moth, in particular, has a life cycle that's intimately tied to the sloth, laying eggs in sloth dung. It sounds strange, but this whole web of tiny creatures living on the sloth may contribute to the algae growth that keeps the sloth hidden and alive. The sloth isn't just an animal — it's a habitat.
